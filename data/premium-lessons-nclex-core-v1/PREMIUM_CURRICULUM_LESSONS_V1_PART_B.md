## LESSON: Seizures
Slug: `seizure-precautions-rescue-meds` (RN); `seizure-observation` (PN/RPN—scope)

### Introduction
Seizures are paroxysmal neuronal discharges; nursing items emphasize **airway protection**, **timing**, **rescue benzodiazepine use per order/protocol**, and **status epilepticus** recognition.

Therapeutic education reduces injury; acute management prevents hypoxic injury and aspiration.

### Overview / Pathophysiology
Excitatory/inhibitory imbalance leads to synchronized firing; prolonged seizures risk neuronal injury and systemic complications.

### Signs and Symptoms
Aura, loss of consciousness, tonic-clonic activity, postictal confusion; subtle: staring, automatisms.

### Danger Signs / Red Flags
Seizure >5 minutes or recurrent without recovery, respiratory compromise, trauma, pregnancy—emergency pathways.

### Labs / Diagnostics
Glucose immediately; sodium/magnesium/calcium as indicated; AED levels if applicable; neuroimaging/EEG per workup—not always acute priorities during active convulsion.

### Nursing Assessment and Interventions
Protect airway (side-lying when safe), loosen clothing, **do not** put objects in mouth; time event; oxygen; prepare rescue meds; neurological checks postictal; seizure precautions per policy.

### Clinical Pearls
**First-line rescue** often benzodiazepines in acute items. **Eclampsia** seizures differ—magnesium pathway in OB stems.

### Client Education
Medication adherence, sleep hygiene, driving laws, injury prevention, when to call EMS.

### Tier-Specific Relevance
**PN/RPN:** observation, safety, escalate; avoid independent med selection beyond order. **RN:** protocol-driven rescue dosing. **NP:** epilepsy management boards.

### Country-Specific Notes
US/CA: scope boundaries differ—follow stem role.

## LESSON: Diabetes Mellitus
Slug: `diabetes-self-management-teaching`

### Introduction
Diabetes mellitus is chronic hyperglycemia from insulin deficiency or resistance. Exams test **hypoglycemia recognition**, **sick-day rules**, **foot care**, and **insulin safety**.

Nursing supports glycemic stability through teaching, monitoring, and coordinated care.

### Overview / Pathophysiology
Type 1 autoimmune beta-cell loss; Type 2 insulin resistance with relative deficiency; both risk microvascular and macrovascular complications.

### Signs and Symptoms
Polyuria/polydipsia, fatigue, blurred vision; hypoglycemia: tremor, diaphoresis, confusion, seizure.

### Danger Signs / Red Flags
DKA/HHS features, persistent vomiting, inability to tolerate PO, altered consciousness—escalate.

### Labs / Diagnostics
A1c for control; glucose POC; BMP for anion gap in illness; microalbuminuria screening contexts.

### Nursing Assessment and Interventions
Capillary glucose checks per order; insulin administration with **five rights**; rotate sites; hypoglycemia protocol; foot inspection education.

### Clinical Pearls
**Basal-bolus** concepts appear; **SGLT2** perioperative hold may show in advanced items.

### Client Education
Carb awareness, foot care, sick-day management, glucagon when prescribed, eye/kidney follow-up.

### Tier-Specific Relevance
**PN:** glucose checks, administer insulin per order, report abnormal values. **RN:** deeper education and care coordination. **NP:** intensification decisions in board cases.

### Country-Specific Notes
US DME teaching vs Canadian community resources—stem provides context.

## LESSON: DKA vs HHS
Slug: `dka-vs-hhs-priorities-hy`

### Introduction
DKA is ketotic metabolic acidosis more common in Type 1 (can occur in Type 2 with stress). HHS is hyperosmolar hyperglycemia with minimal ketosis—profound dehydration and altered mentation.

Exams test **fluid resuscitation mindset**, **insulin cautions**, **potassium shifts**, and **avoiding rapid osmotic shifts**.

### Overview / Pathophysiology
Absolute/relative insulin deficiency raises glucose; osmotic diuresis dehydrates; lipolysis yields ketones in DKA; HHS has extreme hyperosmolarity.

### Signs and Symptoms
Polyuria, polydipsia, vomiting, abdominal pain (DKA); neuro changes more prominent in HHS; Kussmaul respirations in DKA.

### Danger Signs / Red Flags
Potassium derangements, cerebral edema risk with rapid correction in pediatric contexts, hypovolemic shock.

### Labs / Diagnostics
Glucose very high; ABG/VBG for pH/bicarb; anion gap; ketones; BMP for K/Na; osmolality in HHS.

### Nursing Assessment and Interventions
Two large-bore IVs; cardiac monitoring for K shifts; insulin infusion per protocol with hourly glucose checks; frequent neuro checks; strict I&O.

### Clinical Pearls
**Insulin starts only when K is safe** in classic teaching items. **Bicarbonate** reserved for select severe academia per stem.

### Client Education
Sick-day insulin, when to seek care, hydration guidance as appropriate post-stabilization.

### Tier-Specific Relevance
**PN:** frequent vitals, glucose checks, escalate abnormal K. **RN:** protocol execution and education. **NP:** ICU/step-down management vignettes.

### Country-Specific Notes
Same pathophysiology; units metric in CA stems commonly.

## LESSON: Thyroid Disorders
Slug: `thyroid-storm-myxedema-clues`

### Introduction
Thyroid storm is life-threatening thyrotoxicosis; myxedema coma is decompensated hypothyroidism. Items reward **hyperthermia vs hypothermia**, **GI/GU sources of decompensation**, and **avoiding precipitant misses** (infection, surgery, iodine contrast in susceptible patients when stem notes).

### Overview / Pathophysiology
Storm: massive catecholamine sensitivity and hormone excess. Coma: slowed metabolism with hypoventilation and fluid shifts.

### Signs and Symptoms
Storm: fever, tachycardia, agitation, diarrhea, HF. Coma: hypothermia, bradycardia, hypoventilation, altered mentation, hyponatremia.

### Danger Signs / Red Flags
Dysrhythmias, heart failure, respiratory failure—ICU-level care.

### Labs / Diagnostics
TFTs (timing varies acutely); glucose, CBC for infection; ABG if respiratory failure; ECG.

### Nursing Assessment and Interventions
Hemodynamic monitoring; temperature regulation; treat precipitating infection; beta-blockade and antithyroid meds per orders in storm; gentle thyroid replacement in coma with monitoring.

### Clinical Pearls
**Aspirin** may worsen storm in some teaching points (free T4); verify stem. **Infection** as trigger is a classic association.

### Client Education
Medication adherence, symptom recognition, when to seek urgent care.

### Tier-Specific Relevance
**PN:** vitals, glucose, escalate changes. **RN:** ICU monitoring, education. **NP:** outpatient thyroid testing interpretation.

### Country-Specific Notes
Similar exam framing; follow stem lab units.

## LESSON: Acute Kidney Injury
Slug: `aki-prerenal-vs-intrarenal`

### Introduction
AKI is abrupt kidney function decline. Exams contrast **prerenal** (hypoperfusion), **intrinsic** (ATN, nephrotoxic), and **postrenal** (obstruction)—with labs and **FENa/FEUrea** when provided.

Nursing prevents harm by stopping nephrotoxins, monitoring urine output, and avoiding fluid errors.

### Overview / Pathophysiology
GFR drops from perfusion, tubular injury, or outflow obstruction; electrolyte/K+ and acid–base derangements follow.

### Signs and Symptoms
Oliguria, edema, fatigue; uremic: confusion, pericardial friction in advanced items.

### Danger Signs / Red Flags
Hyperkalemia with ECG changes, pulmonary edema, refractory acidosis—emergent dialysis indications in stems.

### Labs / Diagnostics
Creatinine trend; urine studies; imaging if obstruction suspected; electrolytes/ABG.

### Nursing Assessment and Interventions
Strict I&O; daily weights; hold nephrotoxins when ordered; accurate BP; dialysis prep education when chronic transition implied.

### Clinical Pearls
**Contrast-induced** AKI risk factors: CKD, diabetes, volume depletion—hydration per protocol in items.

### Client Education
Medication review, hydration guidance when appropriate, follow-up labs.

### Tier-Specific Relevance
**PN:** measure I&O, report oliguria. **RN:** med reconciliation, dialysis coordination. **NP:** hemodynamic optimization decisions.

### Country-Specific Notes
Creatinine units differ—stem will clarify.

## LESSON: Electrolyte Imbalances (fluids & electrolytes emergencies)
Slug: `fluids-electrolytes-emergencies-gold`

### Introduction
Electrolyte emergencies (K, Na, Ca, Mg, phosphate) appear as **EKG changes**, **neuromuscular excitability or weakness**, and **acid–base** pairing. Items test **replacement routes**, **monitoring frequency**, and **avoiding rapid overcorrection** (especially sodium).

### Overview / Pathophysiology
Cell shifts, renal losses, GI losses, endocrine drivers, and dilution states move electrolytes; cardiac conduction is calcium/potassium/magnesium sensitive.

### Signs and Symptoms
Hypo/hyperkalemia ECG patterns; hypocalcemia tetany; hyponatremia neuro symptoms; hypernatremia thirst/mental status changes.

### Danger Signs / Red Flags
Peaked T waves progressing to arrhythmia (K); osmotic demyelination risk with rapid Na correction—obey protocol ceilings in vignettes.

### Labs / Diagnostics
BMP, ionized calcium, magnesium, phosphate; ABG; ECG serial.

### Nursing Assessment and Interventions
Telemetry when indicated; replacement per protocol; recheck labs after boluses; seizure precautions with severe hypocalcemia; monitor urine output.

### Clinical Pearls
**Mg repletion** helps hypokalemia refractory to replacement. **Loop diuretics** cause K/Mg losses together in items.

### Client Education
Diet counseling when prescribed, adherence to replacement regimens, symptom return precautions.

### Tier-Specific Relevance
**PN:** ECG recognition escalation, lab recheck assistance. **RN:** protocol execution. **NP:** outpatient electrolyte workups.

### Country-Specific Notes
mmol/L labs in Canada—stem provides reference.

## LESSON: Sepsis
Slug: `sepsis-early-recognition-gold`

### Introduction
Sepsis is life-threatening organ dysfunction from dysregulated host response to infection. Early recognition, **source control mindset**, **antibiotic timing**, and **perfusion-focused resuscitation** dominate exam stems.

Nursing is central to **serial assessments**, **lactate/MAP targets when ordered**, and **escalation**.

### Overview / Pathophysiology
Inflammatory cascade leads to vasodilation, capillary leak, and microvascular thrombosis; shock when perfusion fails.

### Signs and Symptoms
Fever/hypothermia, tachycardia, tachypnea, altered mentation, oliguria, mottling.

### Danger Signs / Red Flags
Refractory hypotension, rising lactate, acute kidney injury, respiratory failure—ICU and escalated therapy.

### Labs / Diagnostics
Lactate; cultures before antibiotics when possible but **don’t delay antibiotics** in unstable vignettes; CBC, BMP, coags; imaging for source.

### Nursing Assessment and Interventions
Fluid boluses when ordered; antibiotics fast; frequent vitals; urine output monitoring; glucose control; prepare for pressors.

### Clinical Pearls
**qSOFA/NEWS** may contextualize risk—follow item’s scoring system if given. **Culture timing** vs **antibiotic timing** is a favorite trap.

### Client Education
Infection prevention, vaccination, wound care, when to return for fever/redness.

### Tier-Specific Relevance
**PN:** recognize deterioration, assist with vitals/labs, escalate. **RN:** sepsis bundle elements per protocol. **NP:** source control and antibiotic stewardship in boards.

### Country-Specific Notes
Same Surviving Sepsis principles at high level; institutional protocols vary—stem is law.

## LESSON: Infection Control and Isolation
Slug: `us-rn-infection-control` (see manifest for CA/PN)

### Introduction
Infection control combines **standard precautions** with **transmission-based** precautions (contact, droplet, airborne). Exams test **which precaution for which pathogen**, **PPE sequence**, and **C. difficile contact** specifics.

### Overview / Pathophysiology
Modes: contact, droplet, airborne, common vehicle, vector—each maps to PPE and room engineering controls.

### Signs and Symptoms
Not disease-specific—this lesson is procedural/clinical reasoning.

### Danger Signs / Red Flags
Suspected **TB** or **measles** → airborne isolation until ruled out per protocol; **meningococcal** droplet precautions.

### Labs / Diagnostics
Pathogen identification drives downgrading precautions; surveillance cultures in outbreaks as stem describes.

### Nursing Assessment and Interventions
Hand hygiene; PPE; dedicated equipment; cohorting when indicated; transport plans; visitor education; terminal cleaning for C. diff.

### Clinical Pearls
**C. diff** = contact + soap-and-water handwashing emphasis in teaching. **N95** for airborne—not for plain contact.

### Client Education
Cough etiquette, isolation rationale, adherence to medication for TB when applicable.

### Tier-Specific Relevance
**PN:** PPE application, observation, reporting breaches. **RN:** lead isolation setup, staff education. **NP:** outpatient infection control in some settings.

### Country-Specific Notes
Public health naming may differ; exam stems spell out pathogen and policy.
