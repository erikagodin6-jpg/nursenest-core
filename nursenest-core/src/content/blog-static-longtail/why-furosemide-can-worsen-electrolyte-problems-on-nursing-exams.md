<h2>The exam trap in one sentence</h2>
<p>Furosemide increases urine output, but output is not the same thing as “balanced electrolytes.” Exam items often pair diuresis with <strong>contraction alkalosis</strong>, hypokalemia, hyponatremia risk contexts, or worsening renal perfusion when the stem’s primary problem is not volume overload.</p>

<h2>What the stem is usually testing</h2>
<p>Whether you can match the medication class to predictable effects and the monitoring plan that belongs with it: baseline and ongoing labs where appropriate, assessment for muscle cramps or weakness, dysrhythmia risk context, and patient-specific contraindications implied by the vignette.</p>

<h2>A compact decision lens</h2>
<ul>
  <li>If the patient is decompensating from overload, diuresis may be aligned with the scenario—still watch electrolyte shifts.</li>
  <li>If the patient is hypovolemic, hypotensive, or already critically low on potassium, the “give more diuretic” distractor is often wrong.</li>
  <li>If renal function is tenuous, exam writers may test whether you recognize that aggressive diuresis without addressing perfusion or underlying cause can worsen the clinical picture.</li>
</ul>

<p>Use this lens alongside your pharmacology study loop: class effects first, then stem cues, then the safest nursing action within scope.</p>
