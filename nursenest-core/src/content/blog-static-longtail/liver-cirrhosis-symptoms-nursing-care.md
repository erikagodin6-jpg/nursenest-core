<h2>Clinical overview</h2>
<p>Cirrhosis is end-stage scarring that disrupts synthetic and metabolic functions. Exams emphasize complications of portal hypertension and careful medication dosing in hepatic impairment.</p>

<h2>Symptoms</h2>
<p>Fatigue, jaundice, pruritus, muscle wasting, gynecomastia, palmar erythema, spider angiomas, and bleeding tendency from coagulopathy in teaching clusters.</p>

<h2>Ascites and fluid management</h2>
<p>Daily weights, abdominal girth, sodium restriction education, diuretics as ordered, paracentesis preparation, infection surveillance for spontaneous bacterial peritonitis clues.</p>

<h2>Hepatic encephalopathy</h2>
<p>Asterixis, sleep-wake reversal, confusion; lactulose or rifaximin themes per orders; avoid sedatives; protein moderation per modern teaching when specified in stem.</p>

<h2>Varices and bleed risk</h2>
<p>Soft diet education, avoid straining, recognize melena and hematemesis, large-bore IV access and blood product preparation in acute bleed scenarios per protocol.</p>

<h2>NCLEX-style reasoning pattern</h2>
<p>Stabilize life threats, trend objective data, match interventions to orders and protocols, teach prevention, and escalate when red flags exceed your independent nursing scope.</p>

<h2>Key Takeaways</h2>
<ul>

  <li>Cirrhosis is a multi-organ story: neuro, GI bleed, renal, and infection risks intertwine.</li>

  <li>Never give acetaminophen above ordered limits or combine hepatotoxins casually in exam answers.</li>

  <li>Early recognition of encephalopathy improves safety.</li>

</ul>

<h3>What causes hepatic encephalopathy?</h3>
<p>Gut-derived nitrogenous compounds bypass failing liver detoxification in simplified teaching models.</p>

<h3>Why monitor ammonia?</h3>
<p>It is an adjunct lab in some protocols—not sole determinant of encephalopathy severity.</p>
