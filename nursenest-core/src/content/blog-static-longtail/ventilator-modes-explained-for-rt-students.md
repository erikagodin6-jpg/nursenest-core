<h2>Why Ventilator Modes Matter</h2>
<p>Respiratory therapy students are expected to understand how common ventilator modes support breathing, oxygenation, and respiratory workload. Mechanical ventilation is a major topic in critical care and allied health education.</p>

<h2>Assist Control</h2>
<p>Assist control ventilation provides full ventilatory support for each delivered breath. Patients may trigger additional breaths, but each breath receives the preset support level.</p>
<p>This mode is commonly associated with patients requiring significant respiratory assistance.</p>

<h2>SIMV</h2>
<p>Synchronized intermittent mandatory ventilation combines mandatory breaths with spontaneous breathing opportunities between supported breaths.</p>
<p>Students should understand how SIMV differs from assist control in terms of spontaneous breathing participation.</p>

<h2>Pressure Support</h2>
<p>Pressure support ventilation assists spontaneous breaths with preset inspiratory pressure. It helps reduce work of breathing and is commonly discussed in ventilator weaning concepts.</p>

<h2>CPAP</h2>
<p>Continuous positive airway pressure maintains positive pressure throughout the respiratory cycle to help improve oxygenation and alveolar recruitment.</p>
<p>CPAP still requires spontaneous breathing effort from the patient.</p>

<h2>Ventilator Alarms</h2>
<p>Ventilator alarms require rapid assessment and troubleshooting. Respiratory therapy students should understand the difference between high-pressure and low-pressure alarms and recognize that patient assessment remains the priority.</p>

<h2>ABGs and Ventilator Management</h2>
<p>Ventilator settings are closely connected to ABG interpretation. Carbon dioxide levels reflect ventilation, while oxygenation values reflect gas exchange effectiveness.</p>
<p>RT students should practice connecting ABG patterns with ventilator concepts rather than memorizing isolated numbers.</p>

<h2>Exam Tips</h2>
<ul>
<li>Understand the purpose of each ventilator mode.</li>
<li>Connect ventilator settings with ABG trends.</li>
<li>Separate oxygenation problems from ventilation problems.</li>
<li>Practice mixed ICU and respiratory scenarios.</li>
</ul>

<h2>Key Takeaway</h2>
<p>Ventilator modes are best learned through physiology and clinical reasoning. Understanding how each mode affects breathing support, oxygenation, and patient workload helps RT students perform better on exams and in clinical placements.</p>
