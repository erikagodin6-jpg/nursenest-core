<h2>Understanding Oxygen Delivery Devices</h2>
<p>Respiratory therapy students are expected to understand oxygen delivery systems, oxygenation goals, FiO2 ranges, and patient assessment findings. Oxygen therapy is a foundational topic in respiratory care education and clinical practice.</p>
<p>RT exams frequently test when a specific device is appropriate, how much oxygen it can deliver, and what clinical situations require escalation.</p>

<h2>Nasal Cannula</h2>
<p>Nasal cannulas are commonly used for patients requiring low-flow supplemental oxygen. They are comfortable and widely used in stable patients with mild oxygen needs.</p>
<p>Students should recognize that mouth breathing, high inspiratory demand, and respiratory distress can affect delivered FiO2.</p>

<h2>Simple Face Mask</h2>
<p>Simple face masks provide higher oxygen concentrations than nasal cannulas but require adequate flow to prevent carbon dioxide rebreathing.</p>
<p>They are often used for moderate oxygen requirements when low-flow oxygen is insufficient.</p>

<h2>Venturi Mask</h2>
<p>Venturi masks provide more controlled oxygen delivery and are commonly discussed in COPD management scenarios.</p>
<p>RT students should understand why controlled oxygen concentrations may be useful in patients at risk for hypercapnia.</p>

<h2>Non-Rebreather Mask</h2>
<p>Non-rebreather masks can deliver high oxygen concentrations and are often associated with critically ill patients requiring aggressive oxygenation support.</p>
<p>The reservoir bag should remain inflated during inspiration for effective oxygen delivery.</p>

<h2>High-Flow Nasal Cannula</h2>
<p>High-flow oxygen systems can improve oxygenation and patient comfort while delivering heated, humidified oxygen at higher flow rates.</p>
<p>High-flow systems are increasingly common in acute respiratory care environments.</p>

<h2>Clinical Judgment Example</h2>
<p><strong>Question:</strong> A patient with severe hypoxemia requires rapid high-concentration oxygen delivery. Which device is most appropriate?</p>
<ul>
<li>A. Nasal cannula</li>
<li>B. Venturi mask</li>
<li>C. Non-rebreather mask</li>
<li>D. Room air</li>
</ul>
<p><strong>Correct answer:</strong> C.</p>
<p><strong>Rationale:</strong> Non-rebreather masks are associated with high oxygen concentration delivery in critically ill patients.</p>

<h2>Common Exam Mistakes</h2>
<p>Students often memorize oxygen devices without understanding clinical context. RT exams increasingly emphasize patient assessment, oxygenation trends, and escalation decisions.</p>
<p>Questions may combine respiratory distress, oxygen saturation, ABGs, and oxygen devices in a single scenario.</p>

<h2>Key Takeaway</h2>
<p>Respiratory therapy students should understand not only what oxygen devices are called, but why they are used and how they affect oxygenation in real clinical situations.</p>
