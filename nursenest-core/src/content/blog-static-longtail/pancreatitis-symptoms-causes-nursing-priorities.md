<h2>Clinical overview</h2>
<p>Acute pancreatitis is pancreatic inflammation often from gallstones or alcohol. Exams test recognition, aggressive fluid themes when ordered, nothing-by-mouth status, and monitoring for local complications or systemic deterioration.</p>

<h2>Symptoms</h2>
<p>Severe epigastric pain radiating to the back, nausea and vomiting, fever, abdominal distension, and periumbilical or flank bruising in severe hemorrhagic teaching (Cullen and Grey-Turner signs).</p>

<h2>Causes and labs</h2>
<p>Gallstones, alcohol, hypertriglyceridemia, ERCP, medications, and trauma appear on lists. Lipase elevation supports diagnosis in teaching; leukocytosis and organ failure scores may appear in ICU-style items.</p>

<h2>Nursing priorities</h2>
<p>NPO, IV fluids per resuscitation protocols, pain management per orders, frequent vitals, strict I&O, glucose monitoring, respiratory support if ARDS develops, turn cough deep breathe when not contraindicated.</p>

<h2>Complications to watch</h2>
<p>Pseudocyst, necrosis, infection, hypocalcemia, renal injury, and shock—report worsening pain, fever spikes, or hemodynamic changes.</p>

<h2>NCLEX-style reasoning pattern</h2>
<p>Stabilize life threats, trend objective data, match interventions to orders and protocols, teach prevention, and escalate when red flags exceed your independent nursing scope.</p>

<h2>Key Takeaways</h2>
<ul>

  <li>Alcohol and gallstones dominate exam etiology lists.</li>

  <li>Fluid resuscitation is protocol-driven; nursing monitors response.</li>

  <li>Hypocalcemia and tetany signs can appear in severe pancreatitis teaching.</li>

</ul>

<h2>FAQ Schema Questions</h2>

<h3>Why is NPO ordered?</h3>
<p>Rests the pancreas by reducing secretagogue stimulation in teaching models.</p>

<h3>What lab supports pancreatitis?</h3>
<p>Serum lipase elevation is common teaching; amylase less specific.</p>

<h3>What sign suggests retroperitoneal hemorrhage?</h3>
<p>Flank ecchymosis (Grey-Turner) in classic boards teaching.</p>
