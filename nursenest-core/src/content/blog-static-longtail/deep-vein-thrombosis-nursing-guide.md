<h2>Clinical overview</h2>
<p>Deep vein thrombosis is clot formation in deep veins, often legs. Nursing exams connect immobility, surgery, and hypercoagulable states to assessment, prevention, and safe anticoagulation care.</p>

<h2>Assessment</h2>
<p>Unilateral swelling, warmth, erythema, tenderness along deep veins, pitting edema differences, and calf pain. Homan sign is not relied upon clinically but may appear historically in textbook distractors.</p>

<h2>Prevention</h2>
<p>Early ambulation, sequential compression devices, pharmacologic prophylaxis when ordered, hydration, and TED hose distinctions in institutional policies—match the stem to the ordered intervention.</p>

<h2>Treatment nursing care</h2>
<p>Anticoagulation per protocol, avoid intramuscular injections when appropriate, soft toothbrush education, lab monitoring, limb elevation comfort measures, and marking limb circumference to trend swelling.</p>

<h2>Complications</h2>
<p>PE propagation risk, post-thrombotic syndrome education, and bleeding vigilance—especially peri-procedure bridges in complex items.</p>

<h2>NCLEX-style reasoning pattern</h2>
<p>Stabilize life threats, trend objective data, match interventions to orders and protocols, teach prevention, and escalate when red flags exceed your independent nursing scope.</p>

<h2>Key Takeaways</h2>
<ul>

  <li>Unilateral limb change beats bilateral edema for DVT suspicion in many stems.</li>

  <li>Prevention and treatment are different anticoagulation contexts—read the stem carefully.</li>

  <li>Patient education reduces recurrence and bleeding harm.</li>

</ul>

<h2>FAQ Schema Questions</h2>

<h3>Is Homan sign a reliable test?</h3>
<p>No—modern teaching de-emphasizes it; use objective swelling and imaging when ordered.</p>

<h3>What is mechanical prophylaxis?</h3>
<p>SCDs and ambulation themes to reduce venous stasis.</p>

<h3>Why teach bleeding precautions?</h3>
<p>Anticoagulation increases bleeding risk; early recognition matters.</p>
