<h2>Mechanism in exam language</h2>
<p><strong>Beta blockers</strong> antagonize beta-adrenergic receptors, reducing sympathetic drive to the heart and vessels. Nonselective agents also block beta<sub>2</sub> receptors in lung and liver contexts relevant to exams; <strong>cardioselective</strong> agents preferentially target beta<sub>1</sub> at usual doses but lose selectivity at high doses in teaching models.</p>

<h2>Why patients take them</h2>
<ul>
  <li>Heart rate and blood pressure control in hypertension and angina teaching.</li>
  <li>Post-MI and heart failure neurohormonal blockade in guideline vignettes.</li>
  <li>Rate control for atrial fibrillation when the stem frames that goal.</li>
</ul>

<h2>Side effects that drive NCLEX items</h2>
<ul>
  <li><strong>Bradycardia and hypotension</strong> — assess apical pulse and BP before giving; hold and notify per parameters.</li>
  <li><strong>Bronchospasm risk</strong> — caution with asthma/COPD histories, especially nonselective agents.</li>
  <li><strong>Masked hypoglycemia symptoms</strong> — teach patients with diabetes to monitor glucose closely and recognize sweating without tachycardia.</li>
  <li><strong>Fatigue, depression, sexual side effects</strong> — patient education and reporting thresholds.</li>
  <li><strong>Rebound hypertension</strong> if stopped abruptly — exams mention never abrupt discontinuation.</li>
</ul>

<h2>Nursing teaching script (short)</h2>
<p>“Take at the same time daily; do not skip pulse and BP checks at home if ordered; rise slowly from bed; report wheezing, swelling, dizziness, or fainting; do not stop suddenly without talking to your clinician.” Align wording with the stem’s literacy level.</p>

<h2>Key takeaways</h2>
<ul>
  <li>Know <strong>hold parameters</strong> for pulse and BP.</li>
  <li>Pair beta blockers with <strong>airway history</strong> questions.</li>
  <li>Diabetes teaching must mention <strong>altered hypoglycemia awareness</strong>.</li>
</ul>

<h2>FAQ schema questions</h2>
<h3>Are all beta blockers the same for asthma patients?</h3>
<p>Exam items often flag <strong>nonselective agents</strong> as higher bronchospasm risk; always read the drug name in the stem.</p>
<h3>Why hold a beta blocker for bradycardia?</h3>
<p>Further beta blockade can worsen conduction slowing or cardiac output in the scenario’s severity frame.</p>
<h3>Do beta blockers treat acute bronchospasm?</h3>
<p>No — they are not rescue therapy; items expect bronchodilators for acute bronchospasm unless contraindicated by the vignette.</p>
