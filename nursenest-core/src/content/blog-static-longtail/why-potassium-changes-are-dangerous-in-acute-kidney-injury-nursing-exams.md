<h2>Why exam writers love this pairing</h2>
<p>Acute kidney injury (AKI) reduces the kidney’s ability to balance fluids and electrolytes. On exams, potassium is a frequent “hidden urgency” cue because small changes can accompany large risk when clearance falls and shifts in acid–base status or tissue injury add potassium to the extracellular space.</p>

<h2>What to prioritize in stems</h2>
<p>Look for rising creatinine with oliguria, new ECG changes, muscle weakness, or recent tissue breakdown in the context of reduced urine output. The question is often testing whether you recognize <strong>hyperkalemia as an immediate safety threat</strong> rather than whether you can recite a normal lab range from memory.</p>

<h2>Reasoning pattern you can reuse</h2>
<ol>
  <li>Identify whether the patient is unstable or trending worse (vitals, level of consciousness, work of breathing).</li>
  <li>Connect AKI with mechanisms that raise serum potassium or impair excretion.</li>
  <li>Choose the option that stabilizes emergent risk within nursing scope and triggers appropriate escalation.</li>
</ol>

<p>For structured practice, pair this mental model with your pathway lessons on fluid balance, renal assessment, and electrolyte monitoring, then validate it in the question bank with mixed stems so you practice cue recognition—not pattern matching a single keyword.</p>
