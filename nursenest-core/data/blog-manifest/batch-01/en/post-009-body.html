Digoxin is a cardiac glycoside used less often today but still appears in exam banks and clinical practice. Toxicity can produce cardiac and noncardiac findings. Vision changes and bradycardia are classic teaching pairs because digoxin affects autonomic tone and sodium-potassium pump function in cardiac tissue, and neurotoxicity can show up as visual disturbances.

NCLEX key point: Digoxin increases vagal tone and can slow conduction. Toxic levels predispose to dangerous arrhythmias. Visual changes such as yellow-green halos are a famous exam clue, but not every patient reports them, so do not rely on one symptom alone.

Pathophysiology in plain language

Digoxin inhibits the sodium-potassium ATPase pump in myocytes. That increases intracellular sodium and indirectly increases calcium availability, which strengthens contractility in therapeutic ranges. In toxicity, automaticity and conduction become unstable.

Increased vagal tone slows the SA node and can prolong AV conduction. Bradycardia and heart blocks appear in stems. Arrhythmias can be atrial or ventricular depending on context and electrolytes.

Hypokalemia potentiates toxicity because competing potassium levels affect the pump balance. Hypercalcemia and hypomagnesemia also raise risk in teaching frames. Renal impairment reduces clearance and raises serum levels. Drug interactions matter, including amiodarone and verapamil in many programs.

Central nervous system effects include confusion and visual disturbances. The “halos” story is memorable, but assess broadly when toxicity is suspected.

Signs and symptoms you will see in stems

  • Nausea, vomiting, anorexia, abdominal pain
  • Bradycardia, AV block, PVCs, other arrhythmias
  • Yellow-tinted or halo vision in classic teaching
  • Altered mental status in severe cases
  • Lab clues: elevated digoxin level when measured, electrolyte abnormalities

Nursing interventions (exam framing)

  • Continuous cardiac monitoring when toxicity is suspected or therapy is high risk.
  • Check apical pulse and hold parameters per order before administration in classic teaching.
  • Monitor potassium, magnesium, and renal function as ordered.
  • Teach patients to report GI symptoms, palpitations, or vision changes promptly.
  • Coordinate antidote and advanced therapies per provider and protocol in toxicity scenarios.

Do not adjust digoxin dosing independently. Scope stays within assessment, monitoring, and communication.

NCLEX traps that steal points

  • Ignoring electrolytes: Potassium shifts matter in toxicity risk.
  • Assuming all bradycardia needs atropine: Follow toxicity pathway and orders.
  • Relying only on visual symptoms: Many findings overlap other conditions.

Practice question

Question: A client on digoxin reports nausea and sees colored halos around lights. The heart rate is 48 and irregular. What is the nurse’s priority?

A) Encourage the client to finish breakfast to reduce nausea
B) Assess airway, breathing, circulation, and notify the provider per protocol
C) Administer another dose to stabilize the rhythm
D) Tell the client this is a normal side effect and continue care

Correct answer: B. Suspected toxicity with bradycardia and visual changes needs urgent evaluation. Do not give additional digoxin. Do not dismiss symptoms.

Quick summary

Digoxin toxicity can cause bradyarrhythmias, other arrhythmias, GI symptoms, and visual changes. Monitor electrolytes and renal function, follow hold parameters, and escalate suspected toxicity.
