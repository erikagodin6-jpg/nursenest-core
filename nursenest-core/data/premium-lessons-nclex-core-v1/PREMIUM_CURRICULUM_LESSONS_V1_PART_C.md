## LESSON: Preeclampsia and Eclampsia
Slug: `preeclampsia-vs-eclampsia`

### Introduction
Preeclampsia is pregnancy-related hypertension with end-organ involvement (proteinuria or other criteria per stem). Eclampsia adds **seizures** not explained by other causes. Exams test **BP thresholds**, **magnesium therapy**, **fetal surveillance**, and **HELLP** awareness when labs are provided.

### Overview / Pathophysiology
Endothelial dysfunction and vasospasm produce hypertension, edema, and organ injury; cerebral hyperperfusion risk drives seizure activity.

### Signs and Symptoms
HTN, headache, visual changes, RUQ pain, edema; eclampsia: tonic-clonic activity.

### Danger Signs / Red Flags
Severe-range pressures, thrombocytopenia, elevated liver enzymes, renal dysfunction, fetal distress—escalate OB/emergency pathways.

### Labs / Diagnostics
CBC, CMP, LFTs, uric acid, proteinuria workup; fetal monitoring as ordered.

### Nursing Assessment and Interventions
Strict BP per protocol; magnesium with respiratory/patellar reflex monitoring; seizure precautions; strict I&O; lateral positioning; prepare for delivery timing per team—**follow stem scope**.

### Clinical Pearls
**Magnesium toxicity** sequence: loss of reflexes before respiratory depression in teaching items. **Nifedipine vs magnesium interaction** may appear—stem-dependent.

### Client Education
Warning symptoms, medication adherence, postpartum follow-up for hypertension.

### Tier-Specific Relevance
**PN/RPN:** frequent vitals, assist monitoring, report symptoms. **RN:** magnesium safety and coordination. **NP:** outpatient preeclampsia risk counseling in board cases.

### Country-Specific Notes
Metric BP and lab units common in Canada; same emergency principles.

## LESSON: Postpartum Hemorrhage
Slug: `postpartum-hemorrhage`

### Introduction
PPH is excessive bleeding after birth—primary (first 24h) vs secondary. Causes: **Tone (uterine atony)**, **Trauma**, **Tissue (retained)**, **Thrombin**—exams love **fundal massage**, **uterotonics**, and **transfusion/escalation** timing.

### Overview / Pathophysiology
Uterine contraction compresses spiral arteries; atony fails this mechanism; coagulopathy compounds bleeding.

### Signs and Symptoms
Heavy bleeding, hypotension, tachycardia, fundus boggy/above umbilicus, large clots.

### Danger Signs / Red Flags
Hemodynamic instability, dropping Hgb, signs of DIC—massive transfusion protocols in advanced items.

### Labs / Diagnostics
CBC, coags, fibrinogen; type and screen/crossmatch; VS trends.

### Nursing Assessment and Interventions
Quantify blood loss; fundal massage per protocol; uterotonic meds per order; large-bore IVs; frequent VS; bladder emptying; prepare for OR/interventional radiology when indicated.

### Clinical Pearls
**First-line uterotonic** choices depend on stem contraindications (e.g., hypertension with ergotamine). **Bimanual compression** may appear as temporizing.

### Client Education
Bleeding expectations vs emergency signs, anemia recovery, contraception when appropriate.

### Tier-Specific Relevance
**PN:** assist with fundal checks, measure blood loss pads, escalate. **RN:** lead hemorrhage response. **NP:** risk factor counseling.

### Country-Specific Notes
Blood product policies institutional—stem defines availability.

## LESSON: Pediatric Dehydration & Respiratory Distress (combined site lesson)
Slug: `pediatric-triage-emergencies-gold-standard`

### Introduction
Children compensate then crash quickly. This scoped-gold lesson bundles **dehydration assessment** (mucosa, cap refill, mental status, urine) with **respiratory distress** (retractions, grunting, nasal flaring, silent chest). Exams test **oral rehydration vs IV**, **bronchiolitis/asthma** support, and **when to escalate**.

### Overview / Pathophysiology
Gastroenteritis causes isonatremic losses; respiratory viruses cause lower airway obstruction; both can produce hypoxia and fatigue.

### Signs and Symptoms
Dehydration: delayed cap refill, sunken eyes/fontanelle, dry mucosa, tachycardia. Respiratory distress: accessory muscles, head bobbing, SpO₂ drop, altered interaction.

### Danger Signs / Red Flags
Silent chest, grunting with fatigue, mottling, lethargy—emergency care.

### Labs / Diagnostics
Glucose; BMP if severe; blood gas if respiratory failure; CXR if complication suspected.

### Nursing Assessment and Interventions
WHO/ORT teaching when appropriate; small frequent fluids; oxygen to target; nebulized therapies per orders; frequent reassessment; family support.

### Clinical Pearls
**Febrile infant <28 days** → not “watch at home” when toxic—stems often choose ED evaluation. **Epinephrine for croup** severity per protocol in items.

### Client Education
Oral rehydration technique, return precautions, safe sleep, when to call EMS.

### Tier-Specific Relevance
**PN:** vitals, observe work of breathing, administer ordered therapies. **RN:** triage leadership in acute settings. **NP:** outpatient pediatric board nuances.

### Country-Specific Notes
US/CA immunization and bronchiolitis seasonal patterns—stem supplies context.

## LESSON: Suicide Risk and Mental Health Crisis
Slug: `suicide-risk-assessment-hy` (RN); `suicide-precautions-observation` (PN/RPN)

### Introduction
Exams test **direct questioning** (asking does not plant ideas), **safety planning**, **one-to-one precautions**, and **removing means**. Therapeutic communication and escalation beat documentation-only answers when risk is acute.

### Overview / Pathophysiology
Risk arises from neuropsychiatric illness, impulsivity, substance use, psychosocial stressors—nursing focuses on immediate safety and coordinated care.

### Signs and Symptoms
Hopelessness, intent/plan/means, agitation, intoxication, command hallucinations to self-harm.

### Danger Signs / Red Flags
Stated plan with access, imminent intent, psychosis with self-harm commands, intoxication with agitation—**continuous observation** and provider notification.

### Labs / Diagnostics
UDS when indicated; pregnancy test contexts; metabolic screens as ordered—not always first.

### Nursing Assessment and Interventions
Private but safe environment; remove sharps/belts; continuous monitoring per policy; medication administration; de-escalation techniques; document quotes objectively.

### Clinical Pearls
**Highest priority** is often **safety over finishing paperwork**. **Involuntary hold criteria** vary by region—stem may define.

### Client Education
Crisis hotlines, safety planning, limiting access to lethal means, follow-up appointments.

### Tier-Specific Relevance
**PN:** observation, remove hazards, stay with patient, escalate. **RN:** formal risk assessment tools when ordered, care coordination. **NP:** psychiatric med management in board cases.

### Country-Specific Notes
US/CA legal frameworks differ; examinations usually embed local policy in vignette text.

## LESSON: High-Alert Medications
Slug: `high-alert-medications-safety-gold`

### Introduction
High-alert meds (insulin, anticoagulants, opioids, chemo, neuromuscular blockers) cause disproportionate harm when errors occur. Items test **independent double checks**, **pump programming**, **concentration traps**, and **antidote awareness** (e.g., opioid reversal).

### Overview / Pathophysiology
Narrow therapeutic indices and look-alike/sound-alike risks drive error patterns; systems use barcode scanning and standard concentrations.

### Signs and Symptoms
Bleeding (anticoagulants), hypoglycemia (insulin), respiratory depression (opioids), extravasation (vesicants)—post-administration monitoring.

### Danger Signs / Red Flags
Critical hypoglycemia, active bleeding on anticoagulation, RR depression with opioid—escalate per protocol.

### Labs / Diagnostics
Glucose, INR/aPTT as relevant; opioid toxicity clinical + gas if severe.

### Nursing Assessment and Interventions
Two-nurse checks when policy requires; pump guardrails; teach patient/family red flags; reversal agents per order; document thoroughly.

### Clinical Pearls
**Heparin** vs **enoxaparin** dosing units trip students. **Insulin pen** never share. **PCA** monitoring includes sedation scores.

### Client Education
Medication schedules, bleeding precautions, glucose checks, pain expectations.

### Tier-Specific Relevance
**PN:** participate in checks, observe for adverse effects, report. **RN:** own verification processes. **NP:** prescribing safety in boards.

### Country-Specific Notes
Similar principles; product names may differ slightly—stem specifies drug form.

## LESSON: Pain Management
Slug: `us-rn-pain-management` (see manifest for CA/PN)

### Introduction
Pain management balances **comfort**, **respiratory safety**, and **function**. Exams test **opioid-sparing multimodal plans**, **sedation assessment**, **bowel regimens**, and **avoiding mixed agonist–antagonist traps** in certain populations.

### Overview / Pathophysiology
Nociceptive vs neuropathic mechanisms inform adjuvants; opioids act centrally with tolerance and dependence risks.

### Signs and Symptoms
Pain scales trend; opioid toxicity: sedation, RR depression, pinpoint pupils (context-dependent).

### Danger Signs / Red Flags
Unarousable sedation, RR <10 in items, cyanosis—stop opioid, support airway, reversal per protocol/order.

### Labs / Diagnostics
Renal/hepatic function for dosing; rarely ABG in respiratory depression.

### Nursing Assessment and Interventions
Scheduled + PRN analgesics per orders; nonpharmacologic measures; incentive spirometry after surgery; bowel program with opioids; teach realistic expectations and functional goals.

### Clinical Pearls
**PCA** safety: only patient presses button in standard teaching unless nurse-controlled analgesia specified. **Elderly** lower starting doses.

### Client Education
Pain scales, side effects to report, no alcohol with sedatives, safe storage/disposal of opioids.

### Tier-Specific Relevance
**PN:** administer PRN per parameters, report inadequate relief or oversedation. **RN:** titrate multimodal plans within orders. **NP:** opioid stewardship outpatient cases.

### Country-Specific Notes
Regulatory differences exist; stem provides legal context when needed.
