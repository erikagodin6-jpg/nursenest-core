## LESSON: Acute Coronary Syndrome
Slug: `acute-coronary-syndrome-gold`

### Introduction
Acute coronary syndrome (ACS) is ischemic heart disease that spans unstable angina, NSTEMI, and STEMI. Boards treat ACS as a **time-sensitive safety and prioritization** problem: recognize high-risk presentations (including atypical symptoms in women and older adults), obtain and communicate data quickly, and avoid interventions that worsen hypotension or bleeding.

Clinically, ACS drives morbidity and mortality; nursing’s role is early detection, coordinated reperfusion readiness, and meticulous monitoring during antithrombotic therapy. On exams, expect **ECG timing**, **troponin interpretation in context**, **nitroglycerin contraindications**, **oxygen only when indicated**, and **activation of advanced care** when instability appears.

### Overview / Pathophysiology
Coronary plaque rupture or erosion triggers platelet aggregation and thrombosis, reducing myocardial oxygen supply. Demand–supply mismatch produces ischemic pain and risk of necrosis (MI). Compensatory sympathetic activation raises heart rate and afterload—sometimes worsening ischemia.

### Signs and Symptoms
- **Early:** pressure-like substernal discomfort, anxiety, diaphoresis, dyspnea, nausea; may present as epigastric or jaw/arm radiation.
- **Classic:** crushing pain with diaphoresis; STEMI patterns when ECG is provided.
- **Late / worsening:** hypotension, arrhythmias, sudden altered perfusion—think **shock**, **RV infarct**, or **mechanical complications** in advanced vignettes.

### Danger Signs / Red Flags
Hypotension with inferior MI pattern (RV involvement), new malignant arrhythmias, refractory pain, acute pulmonary edema, or signs of bleeding on anticoagulation require **immediate escalation** and team activation per setting.

### Labs / Diagnostics
Troponins trend with ischemia/necrosis; CBC/coagulation as ordered; BMP for renal impact before contrast; **12-lead ECG** is central when STEMI is suspected. ABG/VBG if respiratory failure.

### Nursing Assessment and Interventions
Continuous cardiac monitoring; frequent vitals; pain reassessment after therapy; strict bleeding checks with anticoagulants; **prepare for reperfusion** (PCI/thrombolysis) as orders dictate; teach **aspirin/ADP inhibitors** cautions when ordered.

### Clinical Pearls
**Inferior MI + hypotension → pause aggressive preload reduction** (NTG) until RV involvement considered. **NTG + recent PDE-5 inhibitor** is a classic contraindication item. **Oxygen** is not automatically correct if saturation is adequate in modern stems.

### Client Education
Heart-attack action plan, medication adherence, when to call EMS, smoking cessation, cardiac rehab referral language as appropriate.

### Tier-Specific Relevance
- **RN:** coordinate diagnostics, administer ordered therapies, monitor for complications, lead team communication.
- **PN/LPN/RPN:** stay with client, obtain vitals, administer ordered meds, recognize red flags, escalate—avoid independent reperfusion decisions.
- **NP:** diagnostic and management framing in primary/acute care boards—still respect item scope statements.

### Country-Specific Notes
- **US (NCLEX-RN/PN):** MONA appears historically; **test current guideline nuance** (oxygen indication, morphine sparing).
- **Canada (NCLEX-RN/REx-PN):** metric labs and interprofessional language; same ACS time principles.

## LESSON: Heart Failure
Slug: `us-rn-heart-failure` (US RN; see manifest for CA/PN)

### Introduction
Heart failure is the inability of the heart to meet perfusion/oxygenation needs at rest or with activity, often with congestion and/or low output. Exams love **volume status**, **medication mechanisms**, and **which patient to see first**.

HF is a chronic exacerbation-prone condition; nursing reduces readmissions through assessment, education, and early escalation for decompensation. Items test **daily weights**, **I&O**, **orthopnea/PND**, **edema patterns**, and **inotrope/diuretic** safety.

### Overview / Pathophysiology
Reduced EF (HFrEF) vs preserved EF (HFpEF) pathways differ, but both can present with congestion. RAAS and sympathetic activation promote fluid retention and remodeling.

### Signs and Symptoms
Early: fatigue, exertional dyspnea. Classic: orthopnea, PND, peripheral edema, JVD, S3. Worsening: hypoperfusion, hypotension, altered mentation, decreasing urine—**cold/wet** framing in advanced items.

### Danger Signs / Red Flags
Resting hypoxia, hypotension with narrow pulse pressure, acute pulmonary edema, chest pain with ECG changes—escalate urgently.

### Labs / Diagnostics
BNP/NT-proBNP context; BMP for renal function/electrolytes; CXR for congestion; echo history often implied; lactate if shock suspected.

### Nursing Assessment and Interventions
Daily weights same scale/time; strict I&O; lung and perfusion assessment; head-of-bed for dyspnea; med administration with **K+/renal** monitoring on diuretics/RAAS drugs; slow position changes.

### Clinical Pearls
**Gain 2–3 lb overnight** often flags fluid retention. Diuretic items pair with **electrolyte** items. Avoid “teach diet” before **assess volume status** when instability is present.

### Client Education
Low-sodium patterns, fluid limits when ordered, weight call parameters, medication adherence, activity pacing.

### Tier-Specific Relevance
**PN/RPN:** measure weights, observe breathing/edema, administer ordered diuretics, report abnormal vitals. **RN:** deeper titration education, coordination, IV diuretics monitoring. **NP:** guideline-directed therapy framing when items allow.

### Country-Specific Notes
US vs CA differs in metric weight teaching; both emphasize **self-monitoring** and **early reporting**.

## LESSON: Dysrhythmias
Slug: `us-rn-dysrhythmias`

### Introduction
Dysrhythmias are disorders of cardiac impulse formation or conduction. NCLEX tests **interpretation tied to action**: stable vs unstable, perfusion, and the **correct sequence** (vagal maneuvers vs adenosine vs cardioversion).

Recognition matters because some rhythms are benign in context while others (VT with pulselessness, VF, unstable tachy/brady) demand **immediate ACLS-aligned responses** as the item defines.

### Overview / Pathophysiology
Re-entry, automaticity, and triggered activity produce rate/rhythm changes; ischemia, electrolytes, meds, and structural heart disease set the stage.

### Signs and Symptoms
Palpitations, dizziness, syncope, chest pain, sudden fatigue; unstable signs: altered mentation, hypotension, pulmonary edema, ischemic pain with rhythm change.

### Danger Signs / Red Flags
Pulseless, severe hypotension, ischemia, acute heart failure with tachyarrhythmia—treat as **unstable tachy** pathways in items.

### Labs / Diagnostics
ECG rhythm strips in stems; electrolytes (K, Mg); troponin if ischemia; thyroid if indicated by vignette.

### Nursing Assessment and Interventions
Continuous monitoring; 12-lead when ordered; vagal maneuvers when appropriate and trained; prepare synchronized cardioversion for unstable narrow/wide complex per protocol in item; medication administration with BP/rhythm checks.

### Clinical Pearls
**Stable narrow-complex SVT** often → vagal → adenosine in many stems. **WPW + afib** is a special-case trap in advanced items. **Torsades** → magnesium.

### Client Education
Trigger avoidance, medication adherence, when to seek care for palpitations/syncope.

### Tier-Specific Relevance
**PN:** recognize instability, obtain vitals, assist with ordered interventions, escalate. **RN:** lead monitoring and emergency med administration per scope. **NP:** broader differential in board vignettes.

### Country-Specific Notes
Terminology consistent; Canadian items may embed metric vitals.

## LESSON: Shock
Slug: `shock-emergencies-gold`

### Introduction
Shock is inadequate tissue perfusion. Exams classify by mechanism—hypovolemic, distributive (septic, anaphylactic, neurogenic), cardiogenic, obstructive—and test **which intervention comes first** given the stem.

Early recognition limits organ damage; nursing priorities are assessment, airway/breathing/circulation support, and **escalation** when perfusion fails despite initial measures.

### Overview / Pathophysiology
Oxygen delivery falls due to loss of volume, pump failure, vasodilation, or obstruction. Compensatory tachycardia and vasoconstriction eventually fail.

### Signs and Symptoms
Tachycardia, altered mentation, cool/clammy vs warm shock states, oliguria, hypotension (late in compensated shock), lactate elevation when presented.

### Danger Signs / Red Flags
Refractory hypotension, widening pulse pressure changes, acute mental status change, anuria—activate emergency pathways.

### Labs / Diagnostics
Lactate, BMP, CBC, cultures if sepsis suspected; bedside ultrasound implied in some advanced items; ECG if cardiogenic.

### Nursing Assessment and Interventions
Large-bore IV access, fluid challenges when ordered and appropriate, pressors per protocol, trend vitals and urine output, head positioning, prepare for transfer to higher care when needed.

### Clinical Pearls
**Septic shock** items pair early antibiotics + source control mindset. **Cardiogenic** may worsen with aggressive fluid blouses—follow stem clues. **Obstructive** (tamponade, PE, tension pneumo) needs **mechanism-specific** fixes.

### Client Education
Infection prevention, medication adherence for HF, allergy action plans when relevant.

### Tier-Specific Relevance
**PN:** recognize shock, stay with patient, assist resuscitation, report. **RN:** titrate therapies per orders/protocols. **NP:** diagnostics/management integration in advanced cases.

### Country-Specific Notes
Same ABC/perfusion principles; scope language differs by role in vignettes.

## LESSON: COPD
Slug: `copd-clinical-judgment-gold`

### Introduction
COPD is progressive airflow limitation often from smoking exposure; exacerbations drive acute care use. Boards emphasize **oxygen titration philosophy**, **infection recognition**, and **avoiding sedation** that blunts respiratory drive.

Nursing reduces harm by monitoring gas exchange, mobilizing secretion clearance support, and catching **impending respiratory failure**.

### Overview / Pathophysiology
Inflammation and parenchymal destruction increase dead space; air trapping causes hyperinflation; chronic CO₂ retention may shift chemoreceptor reliance.

### Signs and Symptoms
Dyspnea, cough, sputum change, accessory muscle use, cyanosis late; exacerbation: increased sputum purulence/volume, worsening dyspnea.

### Danger Signs / Red Flags
Altered mentation in hypercapnia, silent chest, fatigue with rising CO₂—**non-invasive ventilation** may appear in stems.

### Labs / Diagnostics
ABG for acid–base; CBC for infection; CXR to exclude pneumonia/pneumothorax; sputum cultures when indicated.

### Nursing Assessment and Interventions
Bronchodilators per schedule; steroids/antibiotics when ordered; oxygen to target per order (avoid over-oxygenation in chronic CO₂ retainers when stem specifies); incentive spirometry/energy conservation; monitor for infection and delirium.

### Clinical Pearls
**PaO₂ 50–60 mmHg target** may appear in retainers—read stem carefully. **Theophylline toxicity** and **hypokalemia** show up as secondary traps.

### Client Education
Smoking cessation, inhaler technique, exacerbation action plan, vaccination language when appropriate.

### Tier-Specific Relevance
**PN:** administer nebs, observe work of breathing, report worsening mentation. **RN:** ABG interpretation in context, care coordination. **NP:** outpatient COPD guideline items.

### Country-Specific Notes
US/CA both use smoking cessation and oxygen safety; exam units may differ (metric vs imperial) in vitals.

## LESSON: Asthma
Slug: `us-rn-asthma`

### Introduction
Asthma is reversible airway hyperreactivity; acute items focus on **severity classification** (speaking in words, accessory muscles, peak flow) and **priority bronchodilator therapy** with systemic steroids when indicated.

Exams distinguish **routine teaching** from **emergency escalation** when distress escalates.

### Overview / Pathophysiology
Bronchospasm, inflammation, and mucus plugging increase resistance; hypoxemia and hyperinflation worsen work of breathing.

### Signs and Symptoms
Wheeze (may be absent if severe), cough, chest tightness; severe: silent chest, inability to speak, retractions, peak flow plummet.

### Danger Signs / Red Flags
Peak flow <50% personal best, rising CO₂, altered mentation—emergency care and possible intubation per item.

### Labs / Diagnostics
Peak flow/spirometry context; ABG if severe; CXR if complication suspected.

### Nursing Assessment and Interventions
Spacer-optimized inhalers; nebulized bronchodilators; corticosteroids early in exacerbations per orders; oxygen to saturation target; calm positioning; frequent reassessment after treatments.

### Clinical Pearls
**Priority** is often **relieve bronchospasm** before “complete teaching.” **Allergic exposure** triggers vs exercise-induced—different teaching foci.

### Client Education
Trigger avoidance, correct inhaler technique, written asthma action plan, when to call EMS.

### Tier-Specific Relevance
**PN:** assist treatments, observe respiratory fatigue, escalate. **RN:** titrate care in ED/telemetry contexts. **NP:** controller therapy selection in board vignettes.

### Country-Specific Notes
Epi-pen teaching for exercise-induced bronchospasm with anaphylaxis overlap may appear in either country.

## LESSON: Pneumonia
Slug: `us-rn-pneumonia`

### Introduction
Pneumonia is infection/inflammation of lung parenchyma; NCLEX tests **oxygenation prioritization**, **aspiration risk**, **vaccination teaching**, and **sepsis overlap**.

Nursing identifies deterioration early—especially older adults with atypical presentations.

### Overview / Pathophysiology
Alveolar filling impairs gas exchange; systemic inflammation can progress to sepsis.

### Signs and Symptoms
Fever, productive cough, pleuritic pain, tachypnea; older adults: confusion, falls, subtle hypoxia.

### Danger Signs / Red Flags
Hypotension, rising lactate, acute confusion, respiratory failure—escalate sepsis pathways when stem matches.

### Labs / Diagnostics
CXR infiltrates; CBC, procalcitonin sometimes; blood cultures when sepsis; pulse ox continuous.

### Nursing Assessment and Interventions
Oxygen titration; incentive spirometry; mobility as tolerated; antibiotics on time; IS/forced cough support; monitor RR and mentation closely.

### Clinical Pearls
**Immobility + post-op** → atelectasis vs pneumonia distinction. **Aspiration** risk with dysphagia—positioning and speech eval referrals in items.

### Client Education
Finish antibiotics as prescribed (when ordered duration stated), breathing exercises, vaccination, hand hygiene.

### Tier-Specific Relevance
**PN:** vitals, oxygen delivery, oral care/positioning support. **RN:** sepsis recognition, oxygen therapy escalation. **NP:** outpatient vs inpatient criteria in boards.

### Country-Specific Notes
Metric vs imperial vitals; Canadian community-acquired pneumonia contexts may use local guideline names—stem will give needed data.

## LESSON: Stroke & Neurologic Emergencies (includes ICP concepts)
Slug: `stroke-increased-icp-gold`

### Introduction
Ischemic stroke care centers on **time windows** for reperfusion and meticulous neuro checks. Increased intracranial pressure often appears with hemorrhagic pathology, mass effect, or malignant edema—exams test **positioning**, **osmotherapy cues**, and **neuro trend recognition**.

Boards combine **FAST assessment**, **tPA eligibility flags**, and **ICP precautions** (head midline, avoid Valsalva, seizure precautions) depending on vignette.

### Overview / Pathophysiology
Ischemia leads to penumbra salvage opportunity; hemorrhage raises ICP and can herniate. Cerebral perfusion pressure depends on MAP and ICP dynamics.

### Signs and Symptoms
Sudden focal deficits, speech changes, neglect, gaze deviation; ICP: headache, vomiting, Cushing triad in late items.

### Danger Signs / Red Flags
Declining GCS, new pupillary asymmetry, signs of herniation—activate neurosurgical/emergency pathways.

### Labs / Diagnostics
Non-contrast CT to differentiate bleed vs ischemia; glucose; coags/platelets before thrombolysis; BP parameters as protocol dictates.

### Nursing Assessment and Interventions
Neuro checks per frequency ordered; BP management within protocol; NPO until swallow screen; elevate head per order; seizure precautions; avoid tight collars; glucose control.

### Clinical Pearls
**Last known well** time gates reperfusion. **Hypertension management** in ischemic stroke is protocol-specific—don’t improvise in items. **NIHSS** may appear as context.

### Client Education
FAST education, rehab expectations, secondary prevention (antiplatelet, statin, BP control) when appropriate for item.

### Tier-Specific Relevance
**PN:** frequent neuro checks, support ADLs, recognize sudden change and escalate. **RN:** protocol-driven BP/thrombolytic monitoring. **NP:** primary prevention and TIA workups in board cases.

### Country-Specific Notes
US/CA: similar acute priorities; follow each stem’s inclusion/exclusion language for thrombolysis.
