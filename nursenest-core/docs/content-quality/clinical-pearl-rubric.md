# Clinical Pearl Quality Rubric

## Purpose

Clinical pearls are premium teaching assets. They should not restate lesson content or repeat the correct answer. A strong pearl gives the learner a compact insight they can remember during exams, clinical placement, and real shifts months later.

Future generated pearl content remains:

- `published=false`
- `launchReady=false`
- `visibleInNavigation=false`
- `indexable=false`
- `adminOnly=true`

until it passes review and publication gates.

## What A Clinical Pearl Must Teach

A clinical pearl should improve at least one of these learner capabilities:

- Pattern recognition
- Failure-to-rescue awareness
- Medication safety
- Escalation triggers
- Exam shortcuts
- Real-world bedside wisdom
- Transfer of knowledge to a similar but unfamiliar scenario

## Scoring Scale

| Score | Label | Meaning | Gate |
| ---: | --- | --- | --- |
| 1 | Unsafe | Misleading, clinically unsafe, or encourages delay/escalation errors. | Fail |
| 2 | Generic | True but obvious, vague, or simply restates the lesson or answer. | Fail |
| 3 | Acceptable | Clinically relevant but not memorable or practice-changing. | Review |
| 4 | Strong | Specific, useful, safe, and transferable to practice or exam reasoning. | Publish Eligible |
| 5 | Flagship | Memorable, high-yield, practice-changing, and strongly tied to safety or clinical judgment. | Flagship |

## Evaluation Dimensions

Each pearl is evaluated across six dimensions.

| Dimension | What Strong Looks Like | Weak Signal |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Clinical Relevance | Names a real finding, medication, lab, ECG feature, risk, intervention, or patient pattern. | "This is important for safe care." |
| Memorability | Uses concise contrast, pattern, rule, warning, or vivid clinical takeaway. | Long generic teaching paragraph or vague summary. |
| Practical Application | Tells the learner what to notice, monitor, report, hold, reassess, or do differently. | No bedside action or decision implication. |
| Safety Value | Identifies harm, deterioration, escalation, failure-to-rescue, or medication-safety risk. | No consequence if missed. |
| Exam Value | Helps choose between tempting answers, prioritize, delegate, or avoid traps. | "Remember this for the exam." |
| Transferability | Applies beyond the exact item to related patients, shifts, scenarios, or question stems. | Only repeats the current fact. |

## Publish Gates

| Average Score | Gate | Required Action |
| ---: | --- | --- |
| 1-2 | Fail | Rewrite before publication. |
| 3 | Review | Human review and enrichment required. |
| 4 | Publish Eligible | May publish after clinical review. |
| 5 | Flagship | Suitable as an exemplar and marketing-quality educational asset. |

Target: average pearl score >= 4.5 across visible premium pools.

## Reject Patterns

Reject pearls that:

- Restate the correct answer without adding insight.
- Say only that a topic is "important," "priority," or "safe."
- Use "always" or "never" without clinical qualification.
- Encourage delaying assessment, escalation, oxygenation support, hemorrhage control, infection response, or medication safety checks.
- Provide a medication rule without contraindications or monitoring context.
- Teach an exam shortcut that would be unsafe at the bedside.
- Are interchangeable across unrelated questions.

## Strong Pearl Examples

| Topic | Strong Pearl |
| --- | --- |
| Heart Failure | Weight gain warns early, but dyspnea with falling SpO2 means the patient may be deteriorating now. |
| Sepsis | Older adults may skip the fever; new confusion plus hypotension can be the sepsis alarm. |
| Hyperkalemia | A peaked T wave is not just an ECG fact; it is a warning that the myocardium may become unstable quickly. |
| Anticoagulants | A normal-looking patient on heparin can still be bleeding internally; trend hemoglobin, aPTT, bruising, stool, and neuro changes together. |
| Delegation | If the task requires nursing judgment, it stays with the nurse even when the physical task looks simple. |
