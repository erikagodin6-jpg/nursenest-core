# Distractor Taxonomy And Realism Standard

## Purpose

Distractors are teaching assets. A wrong answer should not merely be incorrect; it should expose a realistic learner misconception, workflow mistake, safety risk, or exam trap. Strong distractors make learners explain why the correct answer is best and why every other option is unsafe, premature, delayed, out of scope, or incomplete.

## Distractor Classes

Every distractor should map to at least one class.

| Class | What It Tests | Example Failure Pattern |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Priority Error | Choosing a lower-priority action before airway, breathing, circulation, instability, safety, or acute deterioration. | Teaching before stabilizing a hypoxic client. |
| Timing Error | Doing the right action too early, too late, or without the required preceding assessment. | Documenting before treating hypoglycemia. |
| Assessment Error | Missing the cue, collecting irrelevant data, or failing to reassess after intervention. | Rechecking a sensor while ignoring visible respiratory distress. |
| Safety Error | Increasing harm through delay, contraindication, restraint misuse, infection risk, medication error, or failure to rescue. | Giving insulin to an actively hypoglycemic client. |
| Interpretation Error | Misreading labs, ECG findings, symptoms, trends, or expected versus unexpected findings. | Treating hyperkalemia ECG changes as benign. |
| Scope Error | Assigning, delegating, prescribing, diagnosing, or performing an action outside role or setting expectations. | Delegating unstable assessment to unregulated personnel. |
| Communication Error | Failing to notify, escalate, hand off, clarify, or use therapeutic communication safely. | Reassuring a suicidal client instead of assessing safety directly. |
| Documentation Error | Charting, reporting, or relying on records without assessment, intervention, evaluation, or escalation. | Documenting a fall-risk concern without initiating fall precautions. |

## Why-Tempting Framework

Each incorrect option must explain four things:

1. Why A Learner May Select It
2. Why It Seems Reasonable
3. Why It Is Incorrect
4. What Risk It Introduces

Weak:

> This is incorrect because it is not the priority.

Strong:

> This may seem reasonable because pain is present, but opioid administration before reassessing respiratory rate and sedation can worsen hypoventilation. The risk is delayed recognition of respiratory depression.

## Quality Dimensions

| Dimension | Strong Signal | Failure Signal |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Plausibility | Could realistically attract a learner who notices one cue but misses the priority cue. | Obviously absurd, humorous, or unrelated. |
| Clinical Realism | Fits bedside workflow, assessment sequence, professional role, or care setting. | Action no clinician would realistically consider. |
| Educational Value | Teaches a misconception, trap, or decision rule. | Merely says "incorrect." |
| Differentiation | Has a distinct flaw from other distractors and from the correct answer. | Duplicates another option or differs only by wording. |
| Safety Relevance | Names risk, harm, escalation delay, contraindication, or failure-to-rescue concern. | No consequence if selected. |

## Publish Gate

No question may publish if:

- Any distractor score is below `70`.
- Any distractor fails plausibility.
- Safety analysis is missing.
- Why-tempting analysis is missing.
- Duplicate or throwaway distractors are present.

## Throwaway Patterns

Reject distractors that:

- Are jokes, impossible actions, or non-clinical statements.
- Use `all of the above` or `none of the above`.
- Are obviously unsafe giveaways without a realistic learner trap.
- Repeat the correct answer with a small wording change.
- Rest only on "not the priority" without explaining the clinical reason.
- Do not map to a priority, timing, assessment, safety, interpretation, scope, communication, or documentation error.

## Realism Checklist

Before publication, reviewers should ask:

- Would a real learner plausibly choose this?
- Does the wrong option reflect a common clinical or exam-thinking mistake?
- Is the workflow realistic for the stated role and setting?
- Does the explanation teach what risk the wrong choice creates?
- Are all wrong options meaningfully different from one another?
