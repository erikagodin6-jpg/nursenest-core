# Premium Rationale Contract V2

## Purpose

Rationales are a primary educational asset in NurseNest. A rationale must teach clinical judgment, not merely justify the correct answer. Every published rationale should help the learner understand what to do, why to do it, what not to do, why not to do it, and what can happen if the cue is missed.

This contract applies to RN, RPN/PN, NP, ECG, Labs, Clinical Skills, Pharmacology, Allied Health, Pre-Nursing, Admissions, and future pathway content.

## Required Rationale Structure

Every rationale must include the following elements.

| Element | Requirement |
| --- | --- |
| Correct Answer | Identify the correct answer or correct option set clearly. |
| Why Correct | Explain why the answer addresses the clinical cue, exam task, or learner objective. |
| Why Incorrect | Explain why each plausible distractor is unsafe, lower priority, outside scope, incomplete, or clinically mismatched. |
| Clinical Reasoning | Show the reasoning chain from cues to interpretation to priority action. |
| Patient Safety Implications | Explain potential harm, deterioration risk, escalation need, or monitoring consequence. |
| Clinical Application | Connect the concept to bedside care, placement, simulation, documentation, or professional practice. |
| Exam Strategy | Teach how to approach similar NCLEX, REx-PN, CNPLE, NP, allied, or NGN-style questions. |
| Clinical Pearl | Provide a short, memorable, practice-relevant insight that goes beyond restating the answer. |
| Related Content | Link or reference related lessons, flashcards, skills, labs, ECG activities, simulations, or study plans. |

## Quality Standard

A premium rationale should:

- Name the key cue or finding that changes priority.
- Explain the underlying clinical principle, mechanism, or scope rule.
- Compare tempting wrong options instead of dismissing them.
- Make the safety consequence visible.
- Teach a transferable rule the learner can apply to the next question.
- Use pathway-appropriate language for RN, RPN/PN, NP, Allied Health, Pre-Nursing, or Admissions learners.
- Avoid generic filler, circular statements, and copy-paste templates.

## Unacceptable Patterns

The following patterns require review or rewrite:

- "This is correct because it is the best answer."
- "Option B is incorrect because it is not priority."
- "Use the nursing process."
- "This supports safe care."
- "Follow facility policy."
- "The clinical reasoning is..."
- Repeated sentence stems across many questions.
- Wrong-option explanations that only say "not appropriate," "less important," or "not the priority."
- Clinical pearls that simply restate the answer.

## Publish Gates

Rationales are scored on a 1-100 scale.

| Score | Gate | Action |
| --- | --- | --- |
| <70 | Fail | Do not publish. Rewrite required. |
| 70-84 | Review | Human review and enrichment required before publication. |
| 85-94 | Publish Eligible | Can publish if clinically reviewed and no safety flags remain. |
| 95+ | Flagship | Premium exemplar suitable for high-conversion modules and marketing samples. |

## Scoring Dimensions

The V2 scoring engine evaluates:

- Clinical reasoning depth
- Why-incorrect depth
- Safety teaching
- Clinical application
- Exam relevance
- Transferable learning

Scores should not be treated as proof of clinical accuracy. They are governance signals that identify missing educational depth, repetitive language, and rationales that need human review.

## Rewrite Requirements

Any rationale below 70 must be rewritten to include:

1. The clinical cue that determines the answer.
2. The reasoning chain from cue to action.
3. Why each wrong option is tempting but incorrect.
4. The safety risk if the learner chooses poorly.
5. A clinical application or placement example.
6. An exam strategy note.
7. A specific clinical pearl.
8. Related learning resources.

## Flagship Example Pattern

Use this structure as the target pattern:

```text
Correct Answer:
Administer oxygen and reassess respiratory status.

Why Correct:
The oxygen saturation of 88% with crackles and dyspnea indicates impaired oxygenation. Addressing breathing is the priority before lower-risk comfort or teaching interventions.

Why Incorrect:
Daily weight is useful for monitoring fluid status, but it does not address the immediate hypoxemia. Teaching sodium restriction is important for discharge planning, but the client is currently unstable. Documenting findings without intervention delays needed care.

Clinical Reasoning:
The learner should recognize dyspnea, crackles, and low oxygen saturation as cues of possible pulmonary congestion. ABCs place breathing before education or routine monitoring.

Patient Safety Implications:
Delayed oxygenation support can worsen hypoxemia, increase cardiac workload, and require urgent escalation.

Clinical Application:
At the bedside, the nurse should support oxygenation, reassess breath sounds and SpO2, place the patient upright, and notify the provider if symptoms persist or worsen.

Exam Strategy:
When an answer choice addresses airway or breathing in an unstable patient, compare it against choices that are important but not immediate.

Clinical Pearl:
In heart failure, weight gain may warn you early, but dyspnea and hypoxemia tell you the patient may be deteriorating now.

Related Content:
Heart Failure Lesson; Oxygen Therapy Skill; BNP Interpretation; Heart Failure Flashcards.
```
