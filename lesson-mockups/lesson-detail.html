Overview

Hypertension (HTN) is a chronic condition defined as persistently elevated blood pressure ≥ 130/80 mmHg (ACC/AHA 2017 guidelines) or ≥ 140/90 mmHg (Canadian guidelines). It is one of the most prevalent modifiable cardiovascular risk factors and a leading cause of stroke, MI, heart failure, CKD, and aortic dissection.

The condition is classified as primary (essential) in ~90–95% of cases, with no identifiable single cause, or secondary, where an underlying condition drives the elevation.

Prevalence ~1 in 4 Canadian adults
Risk Stroke, MI, CKD, HF
Goal BP (most adults) < 130/80 mmHg
First-line med class ACE-i / ARB / CCB / Thiazide
Learning Objectives
  • Define hypertension and distinguish primary from secondary HTN
  • Describe the renin-angiotensin-aldosterone system (RAAS) and its role in BP regulation
  • Identify signs of hypertensive urgency vs. hypertensive emergency
  • Explain the mechanism of action, nursing considerations, and patient teaching for first-line antihypertensive agents
  • Apply appropriate nursing interventions for a patient admitted with hypertensive emergency
Pathophysiology

Blood pressure is determined by cardiac output (CO) × systemic vascular resistance (SVR). In essential hypertension, multiple mechanisms converge to chronically elevate SVR:


RAAS Activation: Reduced renal perfusion triggers renin release → angiotensin I → ACE converts to angiotensin II (potent vasoconstrictor) → aldosterone release → sodium and water retention → increased preload and BP.


Sympathetic Nervous System: Chronic SNS activation increases heart rate and contractility (CO↑) and causes arteriolar vasoconstriction (SVR↑). Stress, obesity, and sleep apnea all amplify SNS tone.


Endothelial Dysfunction: Sustained pressure injury damages the endothelium, reducing nitric oxide (vasodilator) bioavailability and promoting atherosclerosis — a self-perpetuating cycle.


Exam Tip: RAAS is the key mechanism targeted by ACE inhibitors and ARBs. Know that ACE inhibitors cause a dry cough (bradykinin accumulation), which is why ARBs are prescribed as an alternative.

Urgency vs. Emergency

Distinguishing hypertensive urgency (≥ 180/120 without end-organ damage) from emergency (same BP with acute end-organ damage) dictates urgency of intervention and setting of care.

Urgency (no EOD)

  • BP ≥ 180/120 mmHg
  • Severe headache
  • Epistaxis
  • Anxiety / diaphoresis
  • No acute organ injury
  • Manage orally (clinic/ED)
  • Target: gradual BP ↓ over 24–48 h

Emergency (with EOD)

  • BP ≥ 180/120 mmHg
  • Encephalopathy / stroke
  • Acute MI / unstable angina
  • Acute LV failure / pulmonary edema
  • Aortic dissection
  • IV antihypertensives (ICU)
  • Target: ↓ MAP by 25% in 1 h

Critical: In aortic dissection, BP must be lowered within minutes targeting SBP < 120 mmHg. Labetalol or esmolol IV are agents of choice — do not use vasodilators (hydralazine, nitroprusside) without beta-blockade first, as reflex tachycardia worsens dissection.

First-Line Pharmacology
Ramipril (Altace)
ACE Inhibitor
MechanismBlocks conversion of Ang I → Ang II; reduces vasoconstriction and aldosterone
Key ADRsDry cough (bradykinin), hyperkalemia, angioedema (rare but serious)
ContraindicationsPregnancy (teratogenic), bilateral renal artery stenosis, angioedema history
Nursing PearlHold if Cr rises >30% from baseline; monitor K⁺. Switch to ARB if cough intolerable.
Amlodipine (Norvasc)
Calcium Channel Blocker (Dihydropyridine)
MechanismBlocks L-type Ca²⁺ channels → arteriolar vasodilation → ↓ SVR
Key ADRsPeripheral edema (ankles), headache, reflex tachycardia, flushing
ContraindicationsSevere aortic stenosis; caution in HF (non-dihydropyridines only)
Nursing PearlAnkle edema is common — elevate legs. Not related to sodium/fluid retention, so diuretics don't resolve it well.
Hydrochlorothiazide (HCTZ)
Thiazide Diuretic
MechanismInhibits NaCl reabsorption in distal convoluted tubule → ↓ extracellular volume
Key ADRsHypokalemia, hyponatremia, hyperuricemia, glucose intolerance
ContraindicationsSulfa allergy, anuria, severe renal impairment (eGFR <30)
Nursing PearlMonitor K⁺ and creatinine. Encourage dietary potassium or co-prescribe K⁺-sparing diuretic if K⁺ falls <3.5.

Clinical Note: Most patients require 2–3 agents to achieve target BP. A common first-step combination is ACE-i/ARB + CCB ± thiazide. Avoid combining ACE-i and ARB (↑ renal risk without added BP benefit — ONTARGET trial).

RPN Nursing Priorities

Assess first: Bilateral arm BPs, neuro status (LOC, pupils, slurred speech), chest pain, SOB, visual changes, urinary output. Identify if emergency vs urgency before any intervention.


  • Position patient in semi-Fowler's; ensure quiet, calm environment to reduce SNS activation
  • Administer antihypertensives as ordered; never hold without provider notification if BP is dangerously elevated
  • Monitor BP every 15 min after new medication or dose change; document trends
  • Restrict sodium intake (≤ 2 g/day); coordinate with dietitian for DASH diet education
  • Assess for orthostatic hypotension before ambulation (especially post-diuretic or new CCB initiation)
  • Reinforce adherence; many patients are asymptomatic and discontinue meds when feeling well
