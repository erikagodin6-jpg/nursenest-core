Item 12

A 68-year-old client is admitted with acute heart failure exacerbation. Assessment: HR 104, BP 158/92, SpO₂ 92% on room air, bilateral crackles, 2+ pitting edema, JVD. Which action should the nurse implement FIRST?

AAdminister furosemide 40 mg IV as ordered to relieve acute volume overload
Why not A
Furosemide is appropriate but not the priority. Address airway and oxygenation (SpO₂ 92%) before pharmacological diuresis.
B Position the client in high-Fowler's position and apply supplemental oxygen at 2–4 L/min via nasal cannula
Correct answer
High-Fowler's reduces venous return and preload, directly relieving pulmonary congestion. Supplemental O₂ corrects the SpO₂ of 92%. ABCs: airway/breathing precede pharmacology.
CObtain a 12-lead ECG and document the cardiac rhythm prior to provider callback
Why not C
Cardiac rhythm assessment is secondary. The immediate priority is hypoxemia and hemodynamic stabilization, not documentation.
DIncrease the IV infusion rate to 125 mL/hr to maintain adequate preload and cardiac output
Why not D
Contraindicated. This client has fluid overload — increasing IV fluids worsens pulmonary edema and dyspnea.
Clinical Rationale Airway & Breathing Priority in Acute HF

In acute heart failure exacerbation with SpO₂ 92%, positioning and oxygenation are the immediate priorities. High-Fowler's (60–90°) reduces venous return to the right heart, decreasing preload and relieving pulmonary congestion. Supplemental oxygen at 2–4 L/min targets SpO₂ ≥ 94%.

Furosemide, while indicated, acts over 30–60 minutes and follows immediate airway stabilization. A 12-lead ECG and IV rate adjustment are appropriate later in the care sequence.

NCLEX Strategy Apply the ABCs (Airway → Breathing → Circulation) and Maslow's hierarchy. Physiological needs, especially oxygenation, precede pharmacological intervention in priority questions.

Item 18

A client with community-acquired pneumonia receiving IV antibiotics has: T 38.9°C, HR 98, BP 136/82, RR 24, SpO₂ 91% on 4 L/min NC. Which nursing interventions are appropriate? Select all that apply.

AElevate the head of the bed to 45° to optimize lung expansion and reduce aspiration risk
Correct — selected
HOB elevation improves diaphragmatic excursion and reduces work of breathing. Correct and appropriate intervention for pneumonia with respiratory compromise.
BApply a non-rebreather mask at 15 L/min for the SpO₂ of 91% on 4 L nasal cannula
Incorrect — selected
SpO₂ 91% on 4L NC warrants titrating to higher-flow NC or simple face mask first. A NRB at 15 L/min is aggressive and not indicated yet — use a stepwise approach.
CEncourage fluid intake of 2–3 L/day to thin secretions and facilitate expectoration
Correct — selected
Adequate hydration thins secretions and facilitates mucociliary clearance. Appropriate for bacterial pneumonia management.
DAdminister acetaminophen 650 mg PO PRN for fever and discomfort as ordered
Correct — you missed this
Acetaminophen reduces fever (which increases metabolic demand and O₂ consumption) and improves comfort. Should have been selected.
ENotify the provider immediately and prepare for intubation based on the current SpO₂
Correctly not selected
SpO₂ 91% does not indicate imminent intubation. Pursue non-invasive oxygen escalation first. Intubation is a last resort after conservative measures fail.
Partial Credit · 3 of 4 correct You missed: Acetaminophen for fever management

You correctly identified HOB elevation, hydration, and avoided aggressive NRB and premature intubation. However, fever reduction (acetaminophen) is an important intervention — fever increases metabolic demand and worsens hypoxemia in pneumonia.

SATA Tip For SATA, evaluate each option independently against the clinical picture. Comfort + supportive care options (antipyretics, positioning, hydration) are frequently correct together in respiratory illness questions.
