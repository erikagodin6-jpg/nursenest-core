Introduction

Clinical Overview

Pulmonary embolism (PE) occurs when a thrombus obstructs pulmonary arterial circulation. Calm mint shell, soft pink accents, readable plum ink — not dense dashboard grey.

Pathophysiology

Mechanism

Obstruction increases right ventricular afterload. Hypoxemia results from ventilation-perfusion mismatch.

  • Thrombus formation in deep veins
  • Embolization to pulmonary arteries
  • Inflammatory cascade
Signs & Symptoms

Presentation

Sudden dyspnea, pleuritic chest pain, tachypnea, and tachycardia are hallmark findings.

Nursing Interventions

Priority Actions

Maintain oxygenation, establish IV access, prepare for anticoagulation per protocol, and monitor for deterioration.

From here down, cards expand under the left Clinical Pearls column — no empty gutter.

Safety Considerations

Red Flags

Assess bleeding risk if anticoagulation is initiated.

NCLEX Pearls

Exam Focus

Sudden dyspnea + clear lung sounds + risk factors = think PE first.
