Overview

What Is a Hypertensive Crisis?

A hypertensive crisis occurs when blood pressure rises severely enough to cause, or risk causing, acute end-organ damage. It is classified into two distinct categories based on the presence or absence of acute target organ involvement.

Hypertensive Urgency
≥ 180/110
Severely elevated BP without acute end-organ damage. Symptomatic but not immediately life-threatening. Oral therapy over 24–48 hours.
Hypertensive Emergency
≥ 180/120
Severely elevated BP with acute end-organ damage. Requires immediate IV antihypertensive therapy. ICU admission.
Pathophysiology

The Cascade of Vascular Injury

When BP rises acutely, the systemic vasculature fails to autoregulate, triggering a cycle of endothelial injury, RAAS activation, and progressive organ hypoperfusion.

Acute BP Surge
Abrupt elevation overwhelms autoregulatory capacity; shear forces damage endothelial cells of arterioles.
Endothelial Dysfunction
Fibrinoid necrosis of arteriole walls → vasoactive mediator release → RAAS & sympathetic activation → sodium retention.
Target Organ Ischemia
Reduced perfusion to brain, kidneys, heart, and eyes with ongoing hypertension-driven injury.
Organ Failure
Hypertensive encephalopathy, AKI, ACS, acute pulmonary edema, or retinal hemorrhage may develop without intervention.
💡
Exam Tip
The differentiator is end-organ damage — not the BP number alone. BP 200/130 without symptoms = urgency; BP 180/120 with neuro changes = emergency.
Signs & Symptoms

Clinical Presentation

Presentation varies by which target organ(s) are involved. Neurological and cardiac symptoms signal emergency status and require immediate escalation.

Urgency — No Organ Damage
  • Severe pulsatile, occipital headache
  • Shortness of breath at rest
  • Epistaxis, palpitations
  • Anxiety, flushing, diaphoresis
  • No acute neurological deficits
Emergency — Organ Damage Present
  • Altered LOC, seizures, confusion
  • Chest pain / ST-segment changes
  • Acute pulmonary edema, crackles
  • Oliguria, hematuria, rising creatinine
  • Visual changes, papilledema
🚨
Red Flags — Escalate Immediately
Call Rapid Response if any of these are present
▸Sudden onset "worst headache of life"
▸New focal neurological deficits
▸Chest pain with diaphoresis or radiation
▸Decreasing level of consciousness
▸Acute pulmonary edema / SpO₂ < 90%
▸Oliguria or anuria (UO < 0.5 mL/kg/hr)
Nursing Interventions

Priority Nursing Actions

Act systematically using A-B-C-S. The goal in emergencies is a controlled 25% MAP reduction within the first hour — not normalization. Rapid drops risk cerebral and coronary ischemia in chronic hypertensives.

1
Continuous cardiac monitoring + BP every 5 min (arterial line preferred)Document BP trending; alert prescriber for changes ≥ 20 mmHg systolic
2
Establish large-bore IV access × 2 (18G or larger)Required for IV antihypertensives and emergency fluid/medication access
3
HOB elevated 30–45°; minimize stimuli; quiet environmentReduces ICP in encephalopathy; positioning promotes venous drainage
4
12-lead ECG, stat labs (BMP, CBC, UA, troponin), hourly neuro checksNIH Stroke Scale if neuro symptoms present; monitor for AKI and electrolyte shifts
5
Administer IV antihypertensive per order; titrate to goal MAPGoal: ≤ 25% MAP reduction in 1 hr → 160/100 by 2–6 hr → near normal by 24–48 hr
🩺
Clinical Pearl
Do not normalize BP rapidly. Chronic hypertensives have shifted autoregulatory curves — over-correction causes cerebral, renal, and myocardial ischemia. Target 25% MAP reduction in 1 hour.

⚡ High-Yield Takeaways
  • Emergency ≠ urgency — end-organ damage is the sole differentiator
  • Target ≤ 25% MAP reduction in the first hour
  • Never give sublingual nifedipine — dangerous BP drops risk stroke
  • Labetalol is first-line in pregnancy (not ACE inhibitors)
  • Nitroprusside requires light-protected tubing; monitor thiocyanate levels
